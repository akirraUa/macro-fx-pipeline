# Transmission Mechanics — How Macro Moves Price

The gap most retail analysis misses: knowing "ECB dovish, Fed hawkish → short EUR" without the chain of *how* it transmits and *when* the effect is exhausted. This file is the channel-by-channel logic.

## Rate differential channel (the core FX driver)

```
CB holds rates high → bond yields stay high → capital flows from
lower-yield region into higher-yield assets → demand for that currency
rises → pair moves accordingly
```

Key: this breaks when the *other* region's yields catch up. Watch the **spread** (e.g. US10Y − DE10Y), not the absolute rate. A narrowing spread weakens the thesis even if neither CB acts.

What the market prices forward matters more than the current print. A CB can be 97% "hold" for the next meeting while the market reprices the *year-end trajectory* toward hikes or cuts — that repricing is the live signal, visible via OIS / Fed funds futures / equivalent.

## The mandate-gauge asymmetry (critical)

The same shock transmits differently depending on which inflation gauge each CB targets:

- **Fed → core PCE** (strips energy/food). Oil moves headline CPI but barely touches core PCE. So an oil shock does *not* strongly move the Fed's reaction function.
- **ECB → headline HICP** (includes energy). Oil feeds directly in. An oil shock *directly* pressures the ECB's mandate.

**Worked example — oil spike:**
- ECB's headline target rises → pressure to hike even into a weak economy (stagflation trap).
- Fed's core target barely moves → Fed can stay on hold.
- Net: divergence narrows (ECB forced to catch up hawkishly).

**Worked example — oil collapse (e.g. ceasefire):**
- ECB's headline target falls fast → the hawkish rationale evaporates → ECB pivots to its weak-growth reality → eases.
- Fed's core target unchanged → Fed holds.
- Net: divergence *widens* in favor of USD.

This is why a structural USD-bullish / EUR-bearish thesis can be **stronger in the oil-collapse scenario than the oil-spike scenario** — counterintuitive, but it follows directly from which gauge each bank targets.

## Risk-flow / safe-haven channel

A geopolitical de-escalation (peace deal) is risk-on:
- Capital exits safe-haven USD → DXY falls → counter-currencies rise short-term.
- This is a *flow* effect, fast and often sharp, but it does not change the rate differential.
- It typically reasserts to the structural story within 1–2 weeks once the flow settles.

Important interaction with positioning: if speculative shorts are crowded (Level 2 COT Index near 0), a risk-on shock squeezes exactly that crowded side — producing a sharp counter-move that is a *squeeze*, not a trend reversal.

## Trade-balance channel (slow, structural)

Oil importer vs exporter matters:
- Eurozone is a net oil importer. Cheaper oil → improved current account → less EUR sold to pay for oil imports → structurally EUR-supportive over months.
- US is a producer; cheap oil is closer to neutral/negative for its trade balance.
This channel is slow (quarters), so it rarely drives the near-term trade but shapes the longer-horizon bias.

## Putting channels together

When multiple channels point different directions, they usually operate on **different horizons**:

| Horizon | Dominant channel | Note |
|---|---|---|
| 1–5 days | Risk-flow (headline reaction) | Sharp, mean-reverting |
| 2–6 weeks | Rate repricing | The structural thesis |
| 3–12 months | Trade balance + growth | Slow bias |

A clean thesis names *which horizon* it's trading and *which channel* dominates there — and flags where a competing channel could cause a temporary counter-move.
